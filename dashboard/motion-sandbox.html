Label motion sandbox

Bird Observatory · Label motion study

Making labels glue to the bird.

The pipeline sees the bird five to eight times a second; the screen refreshes sixty. Without care, the label teleports with every detection. This page is a sandbox for deciding how to bridge the gap.

Everything you'll see is real data — tracks pulled straight from the observatory's pipeline database, looping on the page. Use the top bar to switch between tracks and to compare the smoothed results against the raw observations.

What we have today

Raw pipeline

No smoothing at all. The label is pinned to whichever bounding box just arrived and teleports to the next one ~150 ms later. Every YOLO jitter shows up as a visible flinch. This is the starting point — the problem we're trying to solve below.

Four candidates

Each panel renders the label at a full 60 fps, using a different method to turn the sparse observations above into continuous motion. Hover on any card to lift it.

01

Precision Lock

At every render tick, take a weighted average of all observations in a wide time window — with weights from a symmetric Gaussian centered at "now". Observations close in time count heavily; far ones barely register. Strong noise rejection; the label only moves when the bird genuinely has.

Technique Gaussian kernel smoothing, σ ≈ 0.45 s (half-width ≈ 1.4 s).

02

Organic Glide

Draw a smooth curve directly through the observations — four at a time, with tangents inferred from the neighbouring points. The label passes through every real measurement and travels between them along a C¹-continuous path (no velocity discontinuities at sample boundaries).

Technique non-uniform Catmull-Rom spline, 4-point stencil.

03

Weighted Average

Same idea as Precision Lock, but with a narrower kernel — σ ≈ 0.22 s (half-width ≈ 0.7 s). Still rejects per-frame YOLO flutter, but catches up to real motion noticeably quicker. The trade-off knob between stillness and responsiveness is just σ.

Technique Gaussian kernel smoothing, σ ≈ 0.22 s.

04

Elastic Trail

No lookahead — uses only observations up to "now". The label's position accelerates toward the latest observation like a mass on a spring with viscous damping: always a tick behind, tiny overshoot on settle. Included as the "no-buffer" baseline.

Technique PD controller (stiffness k ≈ 0.12, damping d ≈ 0.75).

A few more in the mix.

Three more approaches worth putting on the table — each bringing an idea the first four don't have. Prediction (we actually model the bird's velocity), adaptive bandwidth (one filter that tunes itself to speed), and local polynomial fitting (preserves the shape of the motion, not just the average).

05

Kinematic Lock

Maintain a running estimate of both the bird's position and its velocity — not just where it is, but how fast and in what direction. When a new observation arrives, weigh it against where the filter predicted the bird would be, and update both numbers. With a velocity estimate, the label can glide between observations instead of jumping to them.

Technique α-β filter (fixed-gain Kalman) with constant-velocity motion model, α = 0.25, β = 0.05.

06

One-Euro

A low-pass filter whose cutoff frequency adapts to the bird's speed. Slow birds get aggressive smoothing (low cutoff, calm label). Fast birds get permissive filtering (high cutoff, responsive label). A single filter, smoothly self-tuning — from HCI research; the de-facto standard for interactive pointer filtering.

Technique Casiez, Roussel & Vogel 2012. fcmin = 1 Hz, β = 0.05, derivative cutoff 1 Hz.

07

Local Fit

Unlike a Gaussian kernel (which inherently blurs the shape of the motion), this fits a local polynomial through the observations in a small window and evaluates it at the current render time. A quadratic captures acceleration; the rendered path matches the bird's actual trajectory more faithfully than any weighted average can.

Technique Savitzky-Golay-style local least-squares fit, degree 2, ±0.4 s window.

The refinement — a proposal

Calm when still, alert when moving.

Precision Lock's weakness is lag on real motion. Weighted Average's weakness is letting jitter through while the bird is still. They're opposite failure modes. The refinement: run both kernels, and at every frame blend them in proportion to how fast the bird is actually moving. The same math, used in the regime where each wins.

Adaptive Lock

Two Gaussian kernels run in parallel: a wide one (σ = 0.45 s) and a narrow one (σ = 0.22 s). The rendered label is a linear blend of their outputs, weighted by the bird's current speed. Standing still → the wide kernel dominates and the label is dead calm. Flying across the feeder → the narrow kernel takes over and the label tracks without lag. Mid-motion → a smooth in-between. You never see it switch.

Technique Two Gaussian kernels (σ = 0.45 s, 0.22 s) linearly combined with blend weight α = clamp((v − 20) / 60, 0, 1) where v is the current speed in px/s, then α itself is low-pass filtered (τ ≈ 100 ms) to avoid regime jitter. Three multiply-adds per axis per frame.

What to watch. The Nuthatch track has a dramatic fly-across around the 6-second mark — that's where the personality of each strategy shows up clearest. Precision Lock smooths the jump into a graceful arc (at the cost of being a tick behind the raw detection). Organic Glide hits the landing exactly but curves through the flight. Weighted Average is the middle ground. Elastic Trail snaps and springs, visibly chasing. Adaptive Lock should look identical to Precision Lock while the bird is still — and then accelerate, following the Weighted Average line once the bird actually moves. Watch the Finch track to see the "still" side of that dial; watch the Nuthatch fly-across to see the "moving" side. The quieter Finch track is the stress test for jitter — watch how much tiny noise leaks through each one while the bird is basically standing still.
